## Q5: What is your leadership style?

### VP-Level Answer:

"I describe my style with concrete examples:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    LEADERSHIP STYLE                                  │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                      │
│   CORE PRINCIPLE: Context over Control                               │
│   ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐   │
│   │ "I provide context and constraints, then trust teams to      │   │
│   │  find the best solutions. My job is to remove obstacles      │   │
│   │  and ensure alignment, not to dictate implementation."       │   │
│   └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘   │
│                                                                      │
│   THREE PILLARS                                                      │
│                                                                      │
│   1. CLARITY                                                         │
│   ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐   │
│   │ What: Clear goals, priorities, and success metrics           │   │
│   │ Example: "Every quarter, I publish team OKRs with explicit   │   │
│   │          rationale and dependencies"                         │   │
│   │ Why: Ambiguity is the enemy of execution                     │   │
│   └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘   │
│                                                                      │
│   2. OWNERSHIP                                                       │
│   ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐   │
│   │ What: Give people end-to-end ownership of outcomes           │   │
│   │ Example: "Engineers own services from design to production,  │   │
│   │          including on-call"                                  │   │
│   │ Why: Ownership drives accountability and growth              │   │
│   └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘   │
│                                                                      │
│   3. FEEDBACK                                                        │
│   ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐   │
│   │ What: Regular, direct, actionable feedback                   │   │
│   │ Example: "Weekly 1:1s focused on growth, not status.         │   │
│   │          I ask 'what's blocking you?' not 'what did you do?'"│   │
│   │ Why: People improve through feedback, not evaluation         │   │
│   └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘   │
│                                                                      │
│   HOW IT ADAPTS                                                      │
│   ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐   │
│   │ Junior team members: More guidance, smaller ownership scope  │   │
│   │ Senior team members: More autonomy, bigger ownership scope   │   │
│   │ Crisis situations: More directive, faster decisions          │   │
│   │ Exploration phase: More collaborative, longer discussions    │   │
│   └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘   │
│                                                                      │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Sample Answer:

"I'd describe my leadership style as 'context over control.'

My job is to provide clear context - the why behind our priorities, the constraints we're operating within, the success metrics we're targeting - and then trust my team to figure out the how. I find that engineers do their best work when they understand the full picture and have ownership of solutions.

Concretely, this means:

**Clarity:** Every quarter, I publish team OKRs with explicit rationale. Not just 'build feature X' but 'build feature X because it unblocks Y business goal and addresses Z customer pain point.' When people understand why, they make better decisions.

**Ownership:** I give people end-to-end responsibility for outcomes, not just tasks. An engineer who owns a service owns it from design through production, including on-call. This builds accountability and growth.

**Feedback:** Weekly 1:1s focused on growth, not status. I ask 'what's blocking you?' and 'what do you want to learn?' rather than 'what did you do this week?'

This style adapts to context. With junior team members, I provide more guidance and smaller ownership scope. In crisis situations, I'm more directive. But the underlying principle stays the same: trust and enable people to do their best work."

### Sample Answer:

"I have a structured approach because there's too much noise to consume everything:

**Daily:** I spend 15-30 minutes on curated feeds - Hacker News front page, a handful of AI researchers I follow on Twitter, and internal Slack channels where teams share what they're exploring.

**Weekly:** I block time for deeper learning. I'll read 1-2 papers in depth, especially if they're relevant to current projects. I also maintain hands-on skills by building small prototypes with new tools.

**Monthly:** I connect with my external network - former colleagues, people from conferences, vendors. These conversations often surface practical insights that don't show up in papers or blog posts.

The key is filtering. Not everything new is relevant. When I evaluate new technology, I ask:
- Is this production-ready or still research?
- Does it solve a problem we actually have?
- What's the maturity curve - should we try now or wait 6 months?

For example, when transformers were new, I was skeptical about using them for tabular data. But when I saw attention mechanisms helping with our pricing model's feature interactions, I dug deeper and we built a production system. The key is being selectively curious - deep on what matters, aware of the rest."

## Summary

Basic behavioral questions at VP level should demonstrate:

1. **Self-awareness**: Know your strengths, weaknesses, and growth areas
2. **Narrative coherence**: Career story should have a logical thread
3. **Strategic thinking**: Even simple questions get thoughtful answers
4. **Evidence-based**: Support claims with specific examples
5. **Authenticity**: Be genuine, not rehearsed

Key patterns:
- Use structured frameworks (STAR, Past-Present-Future)
- Always include specific metrics and outcomes
- Connect your experience to the role's requirements
- Show reflection and continuous improvement
